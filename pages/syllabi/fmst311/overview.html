FMST 311: World Cinema after TV
Course Overview

This course provides an introduction to cinema’s world history as industry, technology, commodity, social practice, and cultural form since the rise of television.
Although electronic televisions had existed since the late 1920s and been commercially available since the late 1930s, it wasn’t until the late 1940s that a widespread
and committed viewership began to form around the medium. By the 1960s, television had surpassed cinema as the most ubiquitous (though not necessarily dominant)
screen medium in the West. This same period witnessed the formation of global distribution networks for the exportation of Hollywood films; the rise of national cinemas
beyond the “Global North”; and the growth of global film markets and transnational productions. This survey is therefore animated by two major questions:
firstly, what impact did television have on film; and secondly, what do we mean by “world cinema”?
